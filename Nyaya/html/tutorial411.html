Negation Normal Forms

A propositional formula in negation normal form does not contain implications, negations of conjunctions or negations of disjunctions. A negation normal form contains negations of atoms, conjunctions and disjunctions.

P  ::=  p  |  ¬p  |  (P ∧ P)  |  (P ∨ P)
a b ¬(a ∨ b) ≡ ¬a ∧ ¬b
T T F = F
T F F = F
F T F = F
F F T = T

Still we do not want to reduce the expressiveness of our formulas. We substitute negations of conjunctions and negations of disjunctions with equivalent formulas. For that we use De Morgan‘s laws.

¬(a ∧ b) ≡ ¬a ∨ ¬b     ¬(a ∨ b) ≡ ¬a ∧ ¬b
a ≡ ¬¬a
T = T
F = F

While substituting implications and negations of conjunctions or dicjunctions we easily get double negations of atoms, which are not allowed in negation normal forms. But double negations can be substituted too.

¬¬a ≡ a

Examples

  • a ∧ b → a ≡ ¬(a ∧ b) ∨ a ≡ (¬a ∨ ¬b) ∨ b = ¬a ∨ ¬b ∨ a
  • a → b → a ≡ a → (¬b ∨ a) ≡ ¬a ∨ (¬b ∨ a) ≡ ¬a ∨ b ∨ a
  • (a → b) → a ≡ (¬a ∨ b) → a ≡ ¬(¬a ∨ b) ∨ a ≡ (¬¬a ∧ b) ∨ a ≡ (a ∧ b) ∨ a
  • ¬a → b ≡ ¬¬a ∨ b ≡ a ∨ b
  • ¬(a → b) ≡ ¬(¬a ∨ b) ≡ ¬¬a ∧ ¬b ≡ a ∧ ¬b

Note that (¬a ∨ ¬b) ∨ b and ¬a ∨ ¬b ∨ a in the first example are equal formulas. We can remove the parenthesis because of syntax conventions. Note that ¬a ∨ (¬b ∨ a) ∨ b and ¬a ∨ b ∨ a in the second example are equivalent formulas. We can remove the parenthesis because we get a semantically equivalent formula.

We observe that negation normal forms looks easier to evaluate.
