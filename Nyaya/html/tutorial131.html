Limits of Propositional Logic

Propositional logic can not handle questions and instructions, but there are other limits too.

All men are mortal

Consider the following classical theorem of logic:

All men are mortal. Sokrates was a man. Therefore, Sokrates was mortal.

This feels right, but in propositional logic we have no argument, because the structure

p. q. Therefore r.

does not hold in all situations (it is possible that I am not late):

The sky is blue. The sea is green. Therefore, I am late.

Predicate Logic

∀x For all x
if
S(x) x is student
→ then
∃y there is some y
I(y) y is instructor
∧ and
Y(x,y) x is younger than y

Propositional logic handles “if ... then”, “and", “or” and “not” quite well. But it does not reflect the finer logical statement of sentences like this:

Every student is younger than some instructor [LICS,93]

We can identify this assertion with a propositional atom p. To express properties (being a student, being an instructor), dependencies (younger than), and quantities (every, some) of entities, we can use predicates and quantifiers in predicate logic.
