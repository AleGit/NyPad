Introduction

Propositions

The basic building blocks of propositional logic are simple propositions – declarative sentences without connectives like “if … then”, “therefore“, “and”, “or”, “not”. These building blocks are called atoms, because like atoms in chemistry, propositions are indecomposable in propositional logic.

We will use small latin letters to represent atoms of propositional logic.

Examples

  • The sum of two and five is seven.
  • The team won its seventh game this year.
  • The sun will shine tomorrow.
  • He reached the bus.
  • I took a taxi.
  • She is late to the meeting.

These sentences are declarative, because they can be checked to be true or false. This checking is not part of propositional logic. We have to use other tools (mathematics, observation, physics, guessing, …) to decide if a proposition is true. For example we have to look at a minimum of three fields at the tic-tac-toe-table to judge who is the winner.

Counterexamples

Not all sentences in english are propositions.

  • What's the sum of three and eight?
  • Did the team win a game this year?
  • Live long and prosper!
  • Read the fine manual!
  • Why did you do that?
  • Don't panic!

Opinions are expressed in declarative sentences, but in many cases they can‘t be checked objectivly, so they are not very suitable to be modelled as atoms of propositional logic.

  • Tom is cooler than Jerry.
