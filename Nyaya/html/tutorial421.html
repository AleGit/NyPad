Conjunctive Normal Form

A literal is an atom or the negation of an atom. A propositional formula in conjunctive normal form (CNF) is a conjunction of clauses, where each clause is a disjunction of literals.

L  ::=  p  |  ¬p
D  ::=  L  |  L ∨ D
P  ::=  D  |  D ∧ P
CNF
a ∨ (b ∧ c) ≡ (a ∨ b) ∧ (a ∨ c)
(a ∧ b) ∨ c ≡ (a ∨ c) ∧ (b ∨ c)

By definition a CNF is implication free and in negation normal form.

To transform a arbitrary formula into CNF, we transform the formula into NNF, then we distribute disjunctions over conjunctions.

Validity

A disjunctive clause of literals is valid (always evaluates to true) if it includes a pair of complementary literals (an atom and the negation of the same atom). A conjunction of clauses is valid if all clauses are valid.

A CNF is valid, if every clause contains a least one pair of complementary literals.
