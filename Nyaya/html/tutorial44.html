Normal Forms

Disjunctive Normal Form

A literal is an atom or the negation of an atom. A propositional formula in disjunctive normal form (DNF) is a disjunction of clauses, where each clause is a conjunction of literals. By definition a DNF is implication free and in negation normal form.

L  ::=  p  |  ¬p
C  ::=  L  |  L ∧ C
P  ::=  C  |  C ∨ P
DNF
a ∧ (b ∨ c) ≡ (a ∧ b) ∨ (a ∧ c)
(a ∨ b) ∧ c ≡ (a ∧ c) ∨ (b ∧ c)

To transform an arbitrary propositional formula into CNF, we transform the formula into NNF, then we distribute conjunctions over disjunctions.

Examples

  • a → b ≡ ¬a ∨ b
  • a ∨ b ∧ c ∨ d = (a ∨ b) ∧ (c ∨ d) ≡ ((a ∨ b) ∧ c) ∨ ((a ∨ b) ∧ d)
    ≡ (a ∧ c) ∨ (b ∧ c) ∨ (a ∧ d) ∨ (b ∧ d)
  • a ∨ b ∧ c ∨ d = (a ∨ b) ∧ (c ∨ d) ≡ (a ∧ (c ∨ d)) ∨ (b ∧ (c ∨ d))
    ≡ (a ∧ c) ∨ (a ∧ d) ∨ (b ∧ c) ∨ (b ∧ d)

Satisfiability

A conjunctive clause of literals is satisfiable (evaluates to true for at least one truth assignment), if it does not contain a pair of complementary literals (an atom and the negation of the same atom). A disjunction of clauses is satisfiable, if at least one clause is satisfiable.

A propositional formula in disjunctive normal form is satisfiable,
if at least one conjunctive clause does not contain a pair of complementary literals.
