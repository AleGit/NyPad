In the first tutorial we identified the simple propositions of two different statements and discovered that the composite propositions have the same structure.

If p and not q, then r. Not r. p. Therefore, q.

This paragraph is build from combinations of simple propositions and connectives. In propositional logic a set of symbols is used to represent these connectives.

Sequents

A sequent is a collection of (composite) propositions (the “premises”) and a collection of (composite) propositions (the “conclusions”) on the right side of “⊢”. We use uppercase latin letters for (composite) propositions (formulas) to distinguish them from simple propositions (atoms).

⊢therefore
P1, …, Pn ⊢ Q1, …, Qm

Our paragraph from above is build from three sentences and one conclusion.

  • If p and not q, then r.
  • Not r.
  • p.
  • Therefore, q.

Using the basic connectives we can write our paragraph as sequent.

To “translate“ natural sentences into atoms or compositions is often ambiguous. We can treat “Elephants are big, grey animals“ correctly as one simple sentence, and represent it with “p” (an atom) or read it as composition “p ∧ q ∧ r” (“Elephants are big and elephants are grey and elephants are animals”).

(p ∧ ¬q → r), (¬ r), (p) ⊢ q

Which can be read as “If the first premise holds, and the second premise holds, and the third premise holds, then the conclusion should hold.“

(p ∧ ¬q → r) ∧ (¬ r) ∧ (p) → q

But we still can‘t prove it.
