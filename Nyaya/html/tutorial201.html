We‘ve already learned that propositional symbols are build form atoms ‘p,q,r,…’ and connectives ‘¬∧∨→’.

atoms p,q,r,…
negation ¬ ¬p “not p”
conjunction ∧ p ∧ q “p and q”
disjunction ∨ p ∨ q “p or q”
implication → p → q “if p then q”

To avoid amibuities we use parenthis in the formal grammer for propositional formulas.

P  ::=  p  |  (¬P)  |  (P ∧ P)  |  (P ∨ P)  |  (P → P)
A propositional formula ‘P’ (on the left side) is defined ‘::=’ through the expression on the right side. The expression of the right side consists of serveral parts, devided by ‘|’, which means or. So ‘P’ is either and atom ‘p’, or the negation of a formula ‘(¬P)’, or the conjunction of two formuals ‘(P ∧ P)’, or the disjunction of two formuals ‘(P ∨ P)’, or the implication of two formuals ‘(P → P)’.

A formal grammar (sometimes simply called a grammar) is a set of formation rules for strings in a formal language. The rules describe how to form strings from the language's alphabet that are valid according to the language's syntax. A grammar does not describe the meaning of the strings or what can be done with them in whatever context – only their form.
